03 Seasonal patterns

Do the patterns move together?

Invisible fleet activity peaks in October, with a secondary surge in July–September. Apparent fishing effort peaks in September. The two rhythms track each other but dark activity shows sharper spikes around the autumn fishery.

Monthly gap events vs. fishing hours, 2017–2019
Dark activity peaks in October, rising 1.6× faster than reported fishing effort. Both series indexed to their seasonal peak = 100%.
Source: GFW gap_seasonality_profile.csv · Data indexed to seasonal peak

AIS gaps are not proof of illegal behaviour. Technical failure, poor satellite coverage, and deliberate deactivation all produce identical signatures. These patterns are signals — not verdicts.

05 Duration vs. scale

Long gaps or many gaps?

Some nations have many short gaps; others have fewer but dramatically longer ones. Spain and Ecuador average over 140 hours per gap nearly six days at a time. China's gaps are shorter but vastly more numerous.

What we can say
Drifting longlines dominate dark activity. Gap events peak in autumn months. Most occur more than 384 km offshore — outside routine patrol zones. Two flags account for over half of all recorded events.
What we cannot say
Whether gaps reflect deliberate concealment or technical failure. Whether fishing occurred during the gap. The legal status of any individual vessel. Causation, intent, or jurisdiction.
The ocean is wide.
The record is not.

This investigation uses publicly available data from Global Fishing Watch covering 2017–2019. The patterns are real. The interpretations are carefully hedged. We name what we measure not what we cannot.
